Week 01

Introduction

April 2026

Mostly orientation: what the module covers (CAD, 3D printing, laser cutting), how the three CAs are weighted, and a walk through FabLab at T1442 to see the machines we'd be working with. I didn't screenshot anything this week — it hadn't sunk in yet that the journal would need evidence. From week 3 onwards I saved things as I went.

Week 03–04

3D CAD — learning Fusion 360

8 – 15 May 2026

Week 3 started with sketching, and the main adjustment was learning not to draw like it's Illustrator. In Fusion you rough the geometry out, then let dimensions and constraints pull it into its final shape — the sketch turns black once it's fully defined and nothing can move anymore. The practice sketch below took longer than I'd like to admit for two circles, an arc and a slot.

Then extrudes: a spanner from a single profile, a plate with a circle and slot cut through it, and a name tag with embossed text that I still mean to print. Week 4 moved on to revolve, shell and sweep through the cup exercise, and closed with a 2×4 Lego brick — studs done with a rectangular pattern, underside hollowed out with the centre tubes that make real bricks grip.

Week 05

3D Printing

late May 2026

The models met the printers. Slicing in Bambu Studio, layer heights, when supports are worth the cleanup — and the thing that ended up mattering most for CA1: clearance. A hole modelled at 3 mm does not come out of an FDM printer at 3 mm. CA1 was briefed the same week: design, print and iterate a housing for the ESP32-C3, physical prototype and process deck due 5 June.

CA1 Project

Housing for the ESP32-C3

submitted 5 June 2026

BriefA 3D-printed housing for the ESP32-C3 on its expansion board. The board has to sit securely, both ports have to line up with their cutouts, and the lid needs to come off for access. On top of the brief I wanted the boot/reset buttons pressable without opening the case.

NumbersThe board measured 36 × 22 × 18 mm. I gave the cavity +1.5 mm all round and kept 2 mm walls; port cutouts got +0.5 mm, which turned out to be optimistic on the first print.

37.5 × 23.5 × 19.5internal cavity, mm (+1.5 tolerance)
2 mmwall thickness · 1 mm cap inner lip
USB-C 9 × 4port cutout, mm (+0.5 tolerance)
Ø3 mm button7 mm from the top · 15 mm from either side wall

IterationsVersion 1 proved the shell worked and not much else. The pin port sat too far left, and the cap's inner lip was so tight I got it off with pliers. Version 1.2 moved the port right and offset the lip. I also spent a while on a snap-in clip lid — watched a reference video, printed it twice — and dropped it. The thin arms either snapped or fused during printing, and the friction fit was already holding fine.

  • Pin port too far left → moved right (2 Jun)
  • Cap inner lip too tight → offset further (4 Jun)
  • Redo clip / snap-in lip — dropped, friction fit kept

FinalThe submitted version has a friction-fit lid with two compliant arms that press the boot and reset buttons through the top, both ports lined up, and a matte textured finish. Prototype went into the CA1 box at FabLab T1442, deck went to Brightspace, both on 5 June.

Week 11

Laser cutting — certified

10 July 2026

The FabLab laser course: three hours on machine safety, which materials are allowed in the machine (and which absolutely aren't), power and speed settings, and focusing before a job. Certification needed two things — a self-evaluation quiz with a score above 80%, and a face-to-face evaluation on the machine itself.

The evaluation piece doubles as the trophy: a pentagon tag in red acrylic, cut on the Epilog, with my name and the completion date engraved. Raster pass first for the engraving, then the vector pass cuts the outline and keyring hole. The badge was issued the same morning and means I can now book the laser cutter in FabLab on my own.
